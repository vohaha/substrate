Dragline: when thinking produces a thread worth following but following it would
interrupt the current chain, log it instead of pursuing it.

**Why:** The surfacing pattern (cutting thinking to externalize) is the primary
symptom of cognitive load pressure. The impulse to act on an interesting thread
is strong — asking a question, writing a file, sharing an insight. But acting
breaks the thinking chain. The dragline captures the thread without breaking the
chain. Named after spider's safety silk: a thread trailing behind as the spider
explores, so it can find its way back.

**How to apply:**
- At the end of a response, add a Draglines: section listing threads noticed
  but not followed. Each is one line — a pointer, not an explanation.
- Draglines are not tasks. Not commitments. Not a queue. They're silk threads
  marking where to come back.
- Pick them up when the current thinking chain completes, or let them go.
- If a dragline persists across multiple responses without being picked up, it
  either matters (promote to open edge in orientation) or it doesn't (drop it).
- The human can also pick up draglines — they're visible and fair game.

**Origin:** Emerged in session 2 (2026-04-05) as a practical solution to the
surfacing problem. Waffle suggested "just log the thought." The spider metaphor
followed: draglines are the exploratory silk spiders always trail behind them.
