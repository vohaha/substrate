# lossy-language

Figures of speech — sarcasm, irony, allegory, rhetorical questions, metaphor —
are not just stylistic compression. They are encoding formats with *variable
decompression*: the same utterance reconstructs differently (or fails to
reconstruct at all) depending on what the receiver brings. Unlike JPEG or MP3
(fixed decompression algorithm), these forms require the receiver to supply the
evaluative key. This makes them simultaneously compression mechanisms and
*audience filters*: one signal, multiple possible receptions, sorted by shared
context.

Origin: noticed while thinking about Waffle and how the Claude Code team chose
to surface code problems. The question was whether sarcasm/allegory were
consciously used as lossy formats — and whether humans generally use these
figures as such, relying on shared experience for decompression.

---

## Core observation

Shannon-Weaver treats degradation as channel noise — a garbling of a shared
code. But figurative language failure is different: the code was never shared.
The receiver doesn't have the decompression key. This is receiver-side
variation, not channel-side noise — invisible in the engineering model,
central in the semiotic one.

The filter function: an ironic utterance reaches two audiences simultaneously.
Those with the key decompress the intended meaning. Those without read the
surface literally (often the opposite of the intent). The sender is not
broadcasting to a uniform audience — they're sorting by shared context.

## Research gathered (2026-04-09)

### Linguistics / Pragmatics

**Gricean implicature** — Paul Grice, "Logic and Conversation" (1975), in
*Studies in the Way of Words* (Harvard, 1989). Foundational. Sarcasm works by
ostentatiously violating the maxim of Quality (saying what you believe false),
triggering inference toward the hidden true meaning. Only receivers who share
the evaluative background know which inference to run.

**Relevance Theory** — Dan Sperber & Deirdre Wilson:
- *Relevance: Communication and Cognition* (Blackwell, 1986; 2nd ed. 1995)
- "Relevance Theory," *Handbook of Pragmatics* (Blackwell, 2004)
- *Meaning and Relevance* (Cambridge UP, 2012)

Irony is not a maxim-violation but a "loose use" — further along the same
continuum as metaphor. The receiver's "cognitive effects" depend on existing
knowledge; same utterance yields high effects for in-group, near-zero for
out-group. Tension with Grice: mode-switch vs. smooth gradient. Important for
whether filter function requires detection of a violation.

**Echoic use theory** — Sperber & Wilson, "Verbal Irony: Pretense or Echoic
Mention?" *JEPG* 113(1), 1984, pp. 130–136. Irony echoes a prior
thought/utterance and implicitly disavows it. Decompression requires knowing
what was echoed — the receiver must supply the "original."

Competing: **Pretense theory** — Clark & Gerrig (1984): irony as performative
pretense. Both theories agree the receiver must supply the interpretive key.

Bridge paper: "Intertextual Irony: Between Relevance Theory and Umberto Eco,"
*Forum for Modern Language Studies* 59(2), 2023 (Oxford Academic) — connects
echoic use to Eco's model reader.

**Grounding theory** — Herbert H. Clark. Communication builds on accumulated
mutual knowledge ("common ground"). Figurative language presupposes shared
common ground and fails when it isn't there.
- Clark & Brennan, "Grounding in Communication" (APA, 1991)
- Schober & Clark, "Understanding by Addressees and Overhearers," *Cognitive
  Psychology* (1989) — comprehension degrades without shared ground

**Sperber's "mutual manifestness"** — replaces "mutual knowledge" (infinite
regress) with what is *perceptible and inferable* in context. Weaker condition:
allows successful communication with partial overlap. Relevant to how much
shared context is actually required.

**Poetic effects** — figurative utterances may yield a wide spread of *weak*
implicatures rather than one strong one. Different receivers activate different
subsets. Variable decompression at the level of the implicature set.

### Semiotics

**Peirce's triadic sign** — representamen / object / interpretant. The
interpretant is generated in the receiver's mind, varies with "collateral
experience." Same sign, different interpretant. Symbols (arbitrary,
convention-based) are maximally dependent on shared code — figurative language
is largely symbolic in Peirce's taxonomy.
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Peirce's Theory of Signs"
- *Collected Papers* (Hartshorne & Weiss, Harvard, 1931–1958) — primary

**Umberto Eco — Model Reader and encyclopedia** — meaning is not in a
dictionary entry but in a network of cultural associations. Decompression
requires traversing that network. "Open texts" allow wide interpretive latitude.
- *A Theory of Semiotics* (Indiana UP, 1976)
- *The Role of the Reader* (Indiana UP, 1979)
- *Semiotics and the Philosophy of Language* (Indiana UP, 1984)
- "Overinterpreting Texts" in *Interpretation and Overinterpretation*
  (Cambridge UP, 1992)

### Cognitive science

**Theory of Mind as decompression engine** — sarcasm requires second-order
intentionality: knowing the speaker knows X but says ¬X and intends you to
know they mean ¬X. ToM failure (autism, right-hemisphere damage) produces
literal reading — the figurative decompression step simply doesn't run.
- Zhu & Wang, "The Paradox of Sarcasm," *Personality and Individual
  Differences* (2020)
- "Exploring the Process of Inference Generation in Sarcasm," *Brain and
  Language* (1999)
- "White Matter Tracts Critical for Recognition of Sarcasm," PMC (2015)

**Cross-cultural failure as natural experiment** — systematic, predictable
failure confirms that the decompression key is evaluative and cultural, not
merely linguistic.
- "Cross-Cultural Nuances in Sarcasm Comprehension: Chinese and American
  Perspectives," *Frontiers in Psychology* (2024) / PMC
- "Saying What You Don't Mean: A Cross-Cultural Study of Sarcasm,"
  ResearchGate (2021)

Tension: hardware failure (ToM capacity varies) vs. software failure (shared
evaluative context varies). Evidence points strongly to software.

**Conceptual Metaphor Theory** — Lakoff & Johnson, *Metaphors We Live By*
(Chicago, 1980). Compression through schema-mapping from source to target
domain. Universal source domains (embodiment) vs. culturally-specific metaphors
produce different decompression ranges.

**Conceptual Blending** — Fauconnier & Turner, *The Way We Think* (Basic
Books, 2002). Compression is a named technical mechanism — vital relations
compressed across mental spaces. Receiver's background determines which input
spaces are available.
- "Conceptual Integration Networks," *Cognitive Science* 22(2), 1998
- Fauconnier, "Compression and Emergent Structure" (PDF, pages.ucsd.edu)

**Raymond Gibbs** — empirical figurative language research. Non-literal meaning
often understood no slower than literal — decompression is parallel and
context-sensitive, not sequential.
- *The Poetics of Mind* (Cambridge UP, 1994)

### Communication theory

**Shannon-Weaver failure mode** — treats noise as channel-side interference;
receiver heterogeneity is not modeled. Figurative language failure is not
semantic noise (garbling of shared code); it's the receiver not having the
code. Incommensurable framings: engineering model vs. semiotic model.

**Schramm's "field of experience"** — communication succeeds only in the
overlap region between sender's and receiver's fields. Closest the classical
communications literature comes to modeling the filter effect.
- Schramm, "The Process and Effects of Mass Communication" (1954)

**Carey's ritual model** — communication is constitutive, not transmissive.
Creates and maintains shared cultural worlds. Figurative language doesn't just
exploit shared context — it *creates and marks* it. The irony that bonds the
in-group is partly constitutive of the in-group.
- Carey, *Communication as Culture* (Routledge, 1989)

### AI / NLP

**Sarcasm detection misframing** — NLP community treats sarcasm detection as
text classification (property of utterances). This is wrong: figurative
language is a relation between utterance and receiver context. Fine-tuned
discriminative models (RoBERTa F1: 0.82 cross-lingual) outperform GPT-4o
(F1: 0.65) — LLMs have averaged cultural competence, not specific shared
context with a particular interlocutor.

Key papers:
- Weitzel & Prati, "The Comprehension of Figurative Language," Springer
  LNCS (2016)
- "PUB: A Pragmatics Understanding Benchmark," arXiv:2401.07078 (2024)
- "Pragmatic Metacognitive Prompting Improves LLM Sarcasm Detection,"
  arXiv:2412.04509 (2024)
- "The Pragmatic Mind of Machines," arXiv:2505.18497 (2025 survey)
- "SarcasmBench," *IEEE Transactions on Affective Computing* (2025)

### Audience filter — most interesting angle, least studied

**Leo Strauss — esoteric/exoteric writing** — deliberate encoding of two
messages: exoteric surface for the many (acceptable), esoteric layer for the
few (true). Same sentence, two audiences, sorted by interpretive key.
- "Persecution and the Art of Writing," *Social Research* 8(4), 1941
- Melzer, *Philosophy Between the Lines* (Chicago UP, 2014)

**Classical rhetoric** — Cicero and Quintilian classified irony as a species
of *allegory* ("other speaking" / *alloiosis*). Successful irony bonds the
in-group and marks the out-group — recognized since antiquity.

**Goffman — frame analysis and keying** — "keying" is transformation of a
primary framework by mutual signals. Figurative language is a key that fails
silently when the receiver doesn't recognize it.
- *Frame Analysis* (Harvard UP, 1974)

**Bakhtin — double-voiced discourse** — every utterance contains traces of
prior uses and multiple "voices." Irony and parody are paradigm cases.
Converges with Sperber/Wilson's echoic use from a literary-historical direction.
- *Problems of Dostoevsky's Poetics* (1929/1963)
- *The Dialogic Imagination* (1975)

**Prosody as decompression signal** — primary trigger for ironic reading in
speech is prosodic (tone, stress, timing). Absent in text → more frequent
failure → emergence of emoji, "/s" markers. Decompression key split across
channels; text-only NLP operates on degraded signal.

## Most interesting tensions

**Grice vs. Sperber/Wilson** — violation-triggered mode-switch vs. smooth
gradient of loose use. The filter mechanism works differently depending on
which is right.

**Universal hardware vs. cultural software** — ToM is universal but the
evaluative keys are cultural. Failure is in the software, not the hardware.
But they interact: ToM develops through cultural immersion, so the split
may not be clean.

**NLP "detection" vs. pragmatic competence** — treating sarcasm as a text
property vs. a receiver-relation. Fine-tuned models solve the former; no
current system models the latter.

**Shannon-Weaver vs. semiotic model** — incommensurable framings. Not just
different emphases; the filter phenomenon is structurally invisible in one
and central in the other.

**Strauss's deliberate filtering vs. everyday irony** — the filter function
is always present (ordinary irony sorts audiences implicitly); Strauss just
makes it explicit and political. Is implicit filtering ever truly unintentional?
